Design Systems
01 Foundations

The material follows the philosophy.

The Constitution defines a workspace that reduces cognitive load and guides attention toward one dominant object. The visual language exists to make that behavior physical. Light, depth, and space are not decoration here — they are the instruments by which the room stays calm and the Hero stays dominant.

Three material decisions carry the entire system, and each answers a specific principle. Every token defined later is an implementation of one of these three ideas.

Depth is hierarchy

Glass tiers translate the attention hierarchy into z-space. The nearer a surface floats, the more it matters. The Hero is closest.

Light is calm

A single periwinkle atmosphere, soft shadows, and low contrast keep arousal low. No pure black, no saturated alerts.

Space is structure

One spatial scale and one grid hold the architecture steady, so the room reads the same on the busiest and quietest day.

Reading rule. Every token in this document lists the Constitution principle it serves. A token that cannot name its principle does not belong in the system — the same subtraction discipline the product applies to features applies here to design decisions.

02 Color Palette

One periwinkle atmosphere.

The palette is a single cool atmosphere with one warm exception. Periwinkle is the environment; deep indigo is the ink; a warm terracotta is reserved — strictly — for the Hero. Color carries meaning through value, not hue: things recede by getting lighter and quieter, never by changing color.

The terracotta law

Warm terracotta is the one hue that draws the eye against the cool field — so it is spent on exactly one thing: the Hero. It never marks a secondary action, a badge, or a status. This single reservation is what keeps “one dominant object” enforceable in color. Serves Constitution §A — exactly one dominant object.

03 Typography

Editorial voice, quiet mechanics.

Two families, each with one job. Hanken Grotesk is the editorial voice — it carries every heading and every sentence, set with generous line-height so prose feels unhurried. Quicksand is the instrument panel — used only for eyebrows, labels, and numerals, always uppercase and widely tracked so it reads as machinery, not narrative. The contrast between the two is what makes the interface feel authored rather than assembled.

Hanken Grotesk
The editorial voice

Headings & body. Weights 300–800. Warm, humanist, confident at large sizes; calm and readable at small ones.

Quicksand
The instrument panel

Eyebrows, labels, numerals. Weights 500–700, uppercase, ≥.14em tracking. Never used for sentences.

Hierarchy by leap, not by crowd

Sizes jump in clear intervals so rank is legible at a glance. A screen shows at most one Display and one Heading — the eye should never have to compare two things of equal weight to find the important one.

Tracking signals role

Display and Heading tighten (negative tracking) to feel composed and intentional. Labels widen dramatically. The reader learns, without thinking, that wide-and-small means “instrument” and tight-and-large means “statement.”

Serves Constitution §7 & §I — progressive disclosure and calm. Restrained type contrast keeps the default surface quiet; depth of information is carried by structure, not by shouting.

04 The Glass System

Three tiers of depth.

Glass is how the workspace expresses spatial hierarchy. Every surface belongs to one of exactly three tiers, and the tier declares how near it floats — and therefore how much it matters. More blur, more opacity, and a longer shadow mean closer to the operator. Three is the whole vocabulary; a fourth tier would blur the ranking the system exists to make clear.

05 Spacing & the Executive Grid

Structure that holds under load.

Space is the architecture’s skeleton. A single 4-based scale governs every gap, and generous room around the Hero is mandatory — whitespace is load-bearing, not leftover. When the day gets heavy the grid does not compress; volume moves into depth instead, so the room keeps its proportions whether it holds three items or three hundred.

06 Motion Choreography

The camera changes focus.

Motion in Executive Tower is a camera adjusting focus, never a room rearranging itself. Transitions are slow, soft, and deliberate — buttery, not snappy. Emphasis cross-fades between tiers; the Hero settles into place with weight. One thing moves at a time, and motion only ever signals a genuine change of state.

Calm Ease
cubic-bezier(.4,.1,.3,1)

Symmetric and unhurried, for opacity and atmosphere. No overshoot, ever — bounce reads as alarm.

“Nothing springs, flashes, or bounces. Emphasis fades in; the Hero settles like a hand set on a desk. If a motion draws attention to itself rather than to a change of state, it is wrong.”

Serves Constitution §9 & §H — adapt emphasis, never change modes. Motion re-weights the same room; it never cuts to a new one.

07 Interaction States

States that reassure, not alert.

Feedback stays inside the periwinkle family. Interaction is confirmed by a gentle rise in light or depth — never by a jump to a saturated “alert” color. The operator should feel acknowledged, not startled. A control that has to shout to be noticed has failed the calm requirement.

08 Governance & Traceability

Every token answers to a principle.
